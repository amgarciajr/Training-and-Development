Director Narrative

Strategic overview for senior leadership — Classification: Internal · Director Distribution · Author: Mike Garcia · June 2026

For Senior Leadership: This section presents the EEP as a strategic investment. Use Director Mode (top-right button) for a clean, full-screen presentation view.
📋 Executive Summary

The Engagement Excellence Program (EEP) is a unified, enterprise-grade delivery operating model designed to make client trust a consistent, engineered output of every engagement. It is not a collection of best practices assembled after the fact, nor a policy document produced in response to a single poor outcome. It is an engineered system built on four interdependent components.

The program was developed by Mike Garcia from approximately eight years of real delivery experience across enterprise clients in regulated industries — including healthcare, energy, financial services, and life sciences. Every element of EEP was field-tested in actual engagements before being codified as a standard.

The Core Problem: Individual engineers routinely deliver excellent technical work. Teams do not consistently translate that work into durable client trust. The gap between delivery quality and client experience outcomes is the program's primary target.
COMMITMENT I

Outcome Ownership

Every engagement has a named Outcome Owner, visible to the client in writing before delivery begins. Trust without accountability is hope.

COMMITMENT II

Communication Discipline

Every client-facing communication passes three quality tests before sending — the Repeat-Back Test, the Stakes Test, and the Next-Step Test.

COMMITMENT III

Validated Value Closure

Every engagement closes with validated client articulation of value received — not task completion, not milestone sign-off.

📊 Two Scenarios: Why Technical Quality Isn't Enough
SCENARIO A — The Escalation That Built Trust

Team detects a critical integration issue mid-project. They immediately notify the client, name an Outcome Owner, communicate the stakes and resolution path clearly, and close the issue on the committed timeline. The engagement experienced a significant problem. The client's trust increases.

SCENARIO B — The Smooth Delivery That Didn't

Engagement completes on time, on budget, to specification. But the client was never clearly oriented to what was being built or why. At closure, the client cannot articulate what they received. No reference is given. No renewal conversation starts.

Program Architecture

Four components, one system — each performs a distinct function, together they constitute the complete operating model

⚡ EDEv4 — The Delivery Engine
Four layers, causally sequenced — each depends on the one before it
1
Signal Intelligence
Weekly monitoring across three signal streams: Customer Friction, Communication Clarity, and Ownership Follow-Through. Signals are monitored weekly — not in post-mortems. Weekly cadence transforms signal intelligence from retrospective to predictive.
2
Ownership Clarity
Every engagement has one named Outcome Owner. Every open issue has one named Outcome Owner. Every escalation has one named Outcome Owner. That name is communicated to the client in writing before delivery begins. The client's experience of accountability is determined by whether they can answer "who owns this?" without ambiguity.
3
Communication Discipline
Three quality tests applied to every client-facing communication: Repeat-Back Test (could the client restate the key content?), Stakes Test (does the client understand what happens next?), Next-Step Test (is the immediate next action crystal clear?). No exceptions for seniority or schedule pressure.
4
Outcome Validation
The standard is not task completion. It is not milestone sign-off. It is client articulation of value received, elicited through three structured validation questions. An engagement that closes with a signed milestone document but no client articulation of value has not closed cleanly under the EEP standard.
🏢 CEEDS — The Team Operating Standard
Four pillars, five operating motions — governs how the team operates day-to-day
THE FOUR PILLARS
Customer Experience Over Technical Perfection — When a choice must be made between technically elegant but confusing vs. technically sufficient but clearly understood, choose clarity.
Clarity Over Complexity — The measure of a communication is not sophistication but comprehension. The standard is always the client's ability to act.
Ownership Without Boundaries — An EEP team member does not leave an issue unaddressed because it falls outside their defined role. Ownership is volunteered, not assigned by default.
Signal Over Opinion — Engagement health is assessed through observable, tracked indicators rather than individual intuition or seniority-weighted opinion.
THE FIVE OPERATING MOTIONS
Weekly Customer Signal Review — Diagnostic session to surface early indicators before they become incidents.
Per-Engagement Ownership Kickoff — Establishes Outcome Owner model at the start of every engagement.
Per-Issue Outcome Owner Assignment — Maintains accountability structure throughout the engagement.
Bi-Weekly CEES Session — Scenario-based practice; the program's primary learning mechanism.
Quarterly Signal Framework Review — Evaluates whether signal streams remain calibrated to current engagement reality.
🏛️ CE Framework — The Client Trust Architecture
The theoretical and architectural foundation — explains which behaviors build trust and why sequencing matters
LAYER 1
Credibility
The client believes we know what we are doing. Established early in the engagement arc — in pre-engagement assessment, kickoff framing, and first deliverable. A team that cannot establish credibility quickly will struggle to recover regardless of subsequent technical quality.
LAYER 2
Reliability
The client can predict how we will behave. Built through consistent follow-through on commitments, predictable communication rhythms, and behavioral consistency under pressure. Converts a positive first impression into an enduring trust relationship.
LAYER 3
Referenceability
The client actively advocates for working with us. The ultimate output of the program — the condition in which the client has experienced sufficient credibility and reliability that they are willing to stake their own professional reputation on recommending the team.
🖥️ Governance Assessment App — The Intelligence Layer
Pre-engagement intelligence that allows all three components to begin from an informed baseline

Before delivery begins, the app establishes a maturity baseline across the client's Microsoft 365 workload environment — capturing governance posture, adoption readiness, and configuration health across the workloads relevant to the engagement scope.

Function 1 — Informed Starting Point: Gives the engagement team the informed starting point they need to calibrate their delivery approach.
Function 2 — Credibility Signal: Gives the client a structured, credibility-building experience of working with a team that arrives prepared.
Key Benefit: Clients who experience a rigorous, data-informed pre-engagement assessment begin the engagement with a materially higher baseline trust level than those who receive a generic kickoff.

Program Charter

Version 1.0 · Effective Q2 2026 · Program Lead: Mike Garcia · Classification: Operational Standard — Mandatory Compliance

Charter Notice: From the date of ratification, the EEP ceases to be an initiative, pilot, or enhancement effort and becomes the expected and enforced standard of delivery excellence for all client-facing engagements.
🚫 The Five Non-Negotiable Commitments
Non-negotiable, non-waivable, non-optional — apply to every engagement without exception
NON-NEGOTIABLE I

Outcome Ownership

Every engagement has a named Outcome Owner, visible to the client in writing, before delivery begins. No engagement may proceed to active delivery without this designation in place.

NON-NEGOTIABLE II

Communication Standards

Every client-facing communication passes the Three Communication Tests before it is sent — no exceptions. Communications that do not pass all three tests are not sent. There is no expedience exception.

NON-NEGOTIABLE III

Value Validation at Close

Every engagement closes with validated client articulation of value received. No engagement is administratively closed without this record in the engagement file.

NON-NEGOTIABLE IV

Runbook Adherence

Every delivery incident follows the applicable Runbook — Escalation, Communication, Handoff, or Incident Response — without improvisation in high-pressure scenarios. The Runbook is the judgment.

NON-NEGOTIABLE V

Operating Cadences

The four operating cadences are non-negotiable standing commitments, not optional enhancements. They are not to be cancelled, deferred, or abbreviated due to schedule pressure or workload.

📖 Four Runbooks
Pre-decided decision frameworks that remove improvisation from high-stakes moments
📞 Escalation Runbook
Defines exactly how an issue is escalated: who is notified, in what sequence, with what information, and on what timeline.
✉️ Communication Runbook
Applies the Three Tests to client communications and defines who approves client-facing messages above a defined sensitivity threshold.
🔄 Handoff Runbook
Governs how engagement ownership transitions between team members or project phases without losing the client's accumulated trust context.
🚨 Incident Response Runbook
Defines the structured response to delivery incidents that carry client relationship risk — including communication sequence, Outcome Owner assignment, and post-incident review.

Signal Intelligence Tracker

Three signal streams — monitor weekly, surface within 48 hours, use signal language not status language

Signal Language vs. Status Language: "I'm seeing a pattern of..." (signal) vs. "Things are going well" (status). Signal language is observable, specific, and actionable. Status language is vague and reactive.

📋 Three Communication Tests — Quick Reference
TEST 1
Repeat-Back Test
Could the client accurately restate the key content of this communication in their own words after one reading?
TEST 2
Stakes Test
Does the client understand what happens next if they do or do not take the requested action?
TEST 3
Next-Step Test
Is the immediate next action crystal clear — who does it, when, and how will completion be confirmed?

Certification Path

Three levels — certification is earned through demonstrated behavior, not course completion

Core Design Principle: "The program certifies what practitioners do in the field, not what they know in the room." Level 3 Trainers certify Level 1 Practitioners — the architecture is built for scale and longevity from the start.
L1
Level 1 — EEP Practitioner
All client-facing team members · Target: 4–6 weeks from onboarding
Entry Level
1
Attend EDEv4 Introduction Session (90 min, live) — Covers the Engagement Delivery Excellence framework foundation, signal stream definitions, and the role of the EEP in active delivery. Async recordings do not satisfy this requirement.
2
Attend CEEDS Onboarding Session (90 min) — CEEDS structure, cadence design, CEES Session format, and documentation protocols.
3
Attend CE Framework Deep-Dive (60 min) — Trust architecture: credibility–reliability–referenceability model, three layers, application to active engagements.
4
Pass Written Scenario Assessment — Two scenarios. Identify standard gaps and articulate correct EEP response. Minimum passing score: 80%. One retake permitted after 72-hour waiting period.
5
Supervised CEES Session Participation — Attend one full CEES Session cycle as participant. Must demonstrate: signal language (not status language), no-blame framing, at least one substantive lesson contribution.
Recognition: EEP Practitioner badge · Invitation to contribute scenarios to CEES Sessions · Addition to program participant record
L2
Level 2 — EEP Lead
Engagement Leads and senior practitioners · Target: 6–9 months from Level 1 · Requires 6+ months active delivery
Leadership Track
1
Active Level 1 Certification — No outstanding coaching flags or unresolved assessment retake situations.
2
Six Months of Delivery Under the Standard — Verified via documented Outcome Owner assignments, signal log entries, and CEES Session participation. Self-reported delivery without corroborating documentation does not satisfy this requirement.
3
Three Playbook Lesson Contributions — Each lesson must include: scenario description, contrast analysis (below-standard vs. at/above-standard response), and a reusable transferable principle.
4
Facilitate One Complete CEES Session Cycle Independently — Four sessions across an eight-week cycle. A Level 3 Trainer observes at least two sessions and provides structured feedback.
5
Pass Level 2 Scenario Assessment — Three scenarios across three performance bands (above/at/below standard). Must correctly identify band, explain behavioral evidence, and reference specific EEP framework elements. Minimum: 80%.
Recognition: EEP Lead designation · Authority to onboard new practitioners · Co-facilitation role in new hire onboarding · Access to Level 2 scenario library
L3
Level 3 — EEP Trainer
Nominated senior leads · Target: 3–6 months from Level 2 · Nomination-based, not universal
Program Steward
1
Active Level 2 Certification — Continued evidence of delivery under the standard during the Level 3 preparation period.
2
Complete the Trainer-of-Trainers Module — Covers: program philosophy, common facilitation failure modes, how to administer Level 1 assessments, how to observe and document participation behavior, and how to handle edge cases in certification decisions.
3
Deliver One Observed Full CEES Session — Scored against the facilitation rubric by the Program Lead or peer Level 3 Trainer. Minimum passing score: 80%. Covers session structure, signal vs. status language discipline, no-blame facilitation, lesson capture quality.

Rating Integrity: Ratings must be supported by observable evidence. A rating of "At Standard" or "Above Standard" requires documentation a second reader can verify — an output, an observation, a dated log entry, or a specific client interaction record.

Scenario 1: The Missed Commitment

Signal Intelligence · Ownership Clarity
You are an Engagement Lead on a mid-size implementation. During your weekly Signal Review, you notice that a deliverable you committed to the client for Tuesday has not been completed — it's now Thursday. The client has not yet raised it. What do you do?
✓ Correct: Option C
The EEP standard requires proactive communication before the client raises the issue. Waiting (A) is a signal failure — the client will eventually notice, and the trust damage from a silent miss is greater than from a transparent one. "Almost ready" (B) fails the Stakes Test and the Next-Step Test — no committed date, no clarity. Escalating internally first (D) delays client communication unnecessarily. Option C demonstrates Ownership Follow-Through at standard: acknowledge, commit to a new date, brief explanation. This is the behavior that builds reliability in the CE Framework trust model.

Scenario 2: The Status Update Email

Communication Discipline
You've drafted a weekly status update email to the client. It covers what was completed this week, what's planned for next week, and notes that "the project is on track." Before sending, you apply the Three Tests. The Repeat-Back Test passes. The Next-Step Test fails — there's no clear action requested from the client. What do you do?
✓ Correct: Option B
The Three Tests apply to every client-facing communication — major or minor. There are no exceptions for "just a status update." Option A is a direct violation of Non-Negotiable Commitment II. Option C fails the Next-Step Test — "please let us know if you have any questions" is not a specific next action; it's a passive invitation that produces no observable client behavior. Option D avoids the problem by eliminating the communication entirely, which is not the standard. Option B correctly applies the test: revise until the communication passes, then send. The revision doesn't need to be elaborate — a simple, specific ask is sufficient.

Scenario 3: The Engagement Close

Outcome Validation
Your engagement is wrapping up. All deliverables have been completed and accepted. The client has signed the milestone completion document. Your manager is asking you to close the engagement in the system so the team can move on. The client has not yet been asked the three validation questions. What do you do?
✓ Correct: Option C
Non-Negotiable Commitment III is explicit: no engagement is administratively closed without a validated client articulation of value received. A signed milestone document is not the same as a client who can articulate what they received and why it mattered. Option A confuses task completion with value validation — this is the exact gap the EEP is designed to close. Option B defers the validation indefinitely — "next quarter" means it will not happen. Option D attempts to route around the standard through seniority — the charter is explicit that seniority does not constitute an exemption. Option C is the only response that upholds the standard. The validation doesn't need to be a long conversation — three structured questions, documented responses, engagement closed.

Scenario 4: The Signal Review

Signal Intelligence · Below vs. At Standard
During your Weekly Signal Review, a team member says: "The client seems happy — no complaints this week, the calls have been going well, and they seemed positive in the last meeting." How do you respond as the Engagement Lead facilitating the review?
✓ Correct: Option B
"The client seems happy" is status language — it is a subjective impression, not an observable signal. The EEP Signal Review is a diagnostic session, not a sentiment check. Option A accepts status language as sufficient — this is the exact pattern the Signal Review is designed to interrupt. Option C introduces a survey process that is not part of the EEP operating model and delays the diagnostic. Option D logs an impression as a signal — this is a calibration failure. Option B is the correct facilitation move: name the language pattern, redirect to the three-stream model, and ask for observable behavioral evidence. "What did the client do or say?" is the right question. The answer to that question is a signal. The impression is not.

🔢 Policy Deployment Sequence — Non-Negotiable Order
1
Verify emergency access account exclusions from ALL existing and new policies before touching anything. Do this first. Do not continue until confirmed.
2
Document the current policy set — export all policies via PowerShell or the Entra portal. This is your rollback baseline.
3
Deploy all new policies in report-only mode. No exceptions. No "just this one" enforcement deployments. Every policy starts in report-only.
4
Run sign-in log analysis for at least 14 days. Do not shorten this window for timeline pressure — 14 days catches weekly patterns a 3-day sample misses.
5
Address high-impact exceptions before enforcement. Document each exception: who approved it, what the business justification is, when it will be reviewed.
6
Enable enforcement for admin group first. Verify for 48 hours. Zero issues before proceeding.
7
Expand by department or role wave. Communicate to users before each wave. Verify for 48 hours before the next wave.
8
Document the final policy set — every policy named, assigned, enforcement status, exclusions, and rationale. This documentation is the client's operational artifact.

Signs of MFA Fatigue Attack in Progress:
• User reporting unexpected MFA prompts — multiple per hour, with no action on their part
• MFA challenge spike visible in sign-in logs for a specific user account
• MFA prompt for an unfamiliar application or an unexpected geographic location
1
Check Identity Protection risk events for the affected user — look for high-risk sign-in flags.
2
Review the user's recent sign-in history — check for impossible travel or unfamiliar IP addresses.
3
If confirmed or suspected: disable the user's active sessions immediately and require a password reset before re-enabling access.
4
Enable number matching on Microsoft Authenticator push notifications immediately if not already active — this is the single most effective control against MFA fatigue attacks.
🏷️ DLP Deployment Sequence
Sequence is everything with DLP. Endpoint DLP is the most disruptive workload — it goes last.
1
Simulation mode for all workloads first. Review match accuracy for a minimum of two weeks. Target a false positive rate below 15% before moving any policy to enforcement.
2
Exchange first — email is your highest-risk exfiltration vector and has the least end-user disruption when a policy blocks a send action.
3
SharePoint / OneDrive second — review access activity and sharing patterns during simulation. Oversharing findings are common and worth a separate conversation with the client.
4
Teams third — validate that Teams DLP does not block legitimate business communications. Pay attention to external guest sharing in Teams channels.
5
Endpoint last. Endpoint DLP is the most disruptive. If you get endpoint DLP wrong, you block users from doing their jobs. Deploy only after the other workloads are stable.
